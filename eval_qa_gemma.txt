  MC selection: **Answer: (B)**
Reasoning:
**(A) misrepresentation:** To establish misrepresentation, there must be a false representation concerning a material existing fact, reasonable reliance, and resulting damages. While the Seller did not *disclose* the issue with the loading docks, there was no affirmative *misrepresentation* of fact. The facts state the problem was not discussed, not that Seller made a false statement. Therefore, this element is missing.

**(B) breach of the covenant of warranty:** A warranty deed includes covenants, including a covenant of warranty. A material latent defect impairs a purchaser’s contractual rights. The issue with the loading docks was a hidden defect (it developed over time with larger trailers) that substantially reduced the value of the property. The Seller knew of this defect (the municipality advised them 90 days before the sale) but did not disclose it to the Buyer. This satisfies the elements of a breach of the covenant of warranty.

**(C) failure of consideration:** Consideration was *not* a failure here. The deed was for “substantial consideration,” meaning the Buyer paid a fair price. A reduction in value *after* the sale does not equate to a failure of consideration. This element is missing.

**(D) mutual mistake:** A mutual mistake requires both parties to be mistaken about the same material fact at the time of the contract. Here, the Seller knew about the issue with the loading docks, and the problem was not discussed with the Buyer. This is not a mutual mistake; it’s a unilateral knowledge disparity. Therefore, this element is missing.
