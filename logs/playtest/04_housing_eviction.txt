**Issue**: What are the legal grounds, procedural notice requirements, and available tenant defenses for a landlord to lawfully evict a tenant in California?

**Rule**: In California, a landlord may recover possession only through an unlawful detainer action, a summary proceeding where the sole issue is the right to possession [Evidence 7]. The statutes governing this action must be strictly followed [Evidence 8]. Valid grounds for eviction are limited to specific reasons authorized by law [Evidence 1]. The primary statutory grounds include: (1) default in the payment of rent; (2) commission of waste that permanently damages the property's market value; and (3) using the property for an illegal purpose [Evidence 6][Evidence 7]. A landlord may also evict a tenant under the Ellis Act to withdraw the property from the rental market, provided they comply with the Act's requirements [Evidence 2], or choose not to renew an expired lease [Evidence 5].

**Application**: The procedural requirements and available defenses depend on the ground for eviction. For the most common action—nonpayment of rent—the landlord must prove five elements: (1) the tenant is in possession; (2) possession is without the landlord's permission; (3) the tenant is in default for non-payment; (4) the tenant was properly served with a written three-day notice stating the amount due; and (5) the default continues after the notice period expires [Evidence 6][Evidence 7]. Strict compliance with the three-day notice requirement is essential to prevail [Evidence 8]. For other grounds like lease violations, the research did not specify the precise notice period, but landlords must give a "Notice to Quit" that states the reason for eviction and what the tenant can do to avoid it [Evidence 5].

Tenants have multiple affirmative defenses available. A tenant may raise violations of a local rent control or eviction ordinance as a defense [Evidence 1]. Specific defenses to a nonpayment action include showing the landlord breached the warranty of habitability [Evidence 3] or acted in bad faith by refusing to accept a timely tender of rent to manufacture a default [Evidence 4]. The defense of frustration of purpose is also available [Evidence 4]. Tenants may assert retaliatory eviction if the landlord acts because the tenant reported code violations or engaged in other protected activity [Evidence 5]. In an Ellis Act eviction, a tenant may defend by showing the owner did not have a bona fide intent to withdraw from the rental business [Evidence 2] or, for an elderly or disabled tenant, that the owner failed to continue the tenancy on the same terms and conditions during the notice period [Evidence 3]. The research did not address defenses specific to evictions for waste or illegal use.

**Conclusion**: A California landlord may evict a tenant only for specific statutory grounds, primarily nonpayment of rent, waste, or illegal use, by initiating an unlawful detainer action and providing strict statutory notice. Tenants possess several affirmative defenses, including habitability breaches, landlord bad faith, retaliation, and violations of local ordinances or the Ellis Act's specific requirements.
