**Issue**: Whether the Supreme Court's decision in *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo* overruled the *Chevron* deference doctrine and, if so, what effect this has on existing administrative law precedents.

**Rule**: The *Chevron* deference doctrine, established in 1984, required courts to defer to a federal agency's reasonable interpretation of an ambiguous statute it administers [Evidence 5]. This involved a two-step framework: first, determining if Congress's intent was clear; if not, the court would defer to any permissible agency construction [Evidence 1][Evidence 3]. A threshold "step zero" inquiry determined whether *Chevron* applied based on congressional delegation and the formality of the agency's action [Evidence 2]. Where *Chevron* did not apply, courts could give an agency's interpretation persuasive weight under the *Skidmore* standard [Evidence 2][Evidence 5]. The principle of *stare decisis* generally obligates courts to adhere to precedent to promote predictability and reliance, requiring "special justification" for overruling, such as a precedent being unworkable or badly reasoned [Evidence 14][Evidence 16][Evidence 17].

**Application**: In *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo*, the Supreme Court held that the *Chevron* framework violated Section 706 of the Administrative Procedure Act (APA) [Evidence 8][Evidence 11]. The Court ruled that the APA requires courts to exercise their independent judgment in deciding whether an agency has acted within its statutory authority and that courts may not defer to an agency's interpretation simply because a statute is ambiguous [Evidence 11]. Consequently, the Court explicitly overruled the 1984 precedent of *Chevron U.S.A., Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc.* [Evidence 6][Evidence 10][Evidence 11]. In place of *Chevron*, courts must now use their independent judgment to determine statutory meaning, though they may still give executive agency expertise consideration under the weaker *Skidmore* standard, where an interpretation's persuasiveness determines its weight [Evidence 11][Evidence 12].

Regarding the effect on existing precedents, the Court's decision applies prospectively. The holding states that "existing rules and case law already decided under *Chevron* deference were to remain in place" [Evidence 11]. This means prior judicial decisions that relied on *Chevron* are not automatically invalidated. However, the overruling of such a foundational doctrine suggests a significant shift in the interpretive methodology courts must apply going forward. The Court's action aligns with recognized justifications for departing from *stare decisis*, as it found *Chevron* to be inconsistent with the APA's command for independent judicial judgment—a rationale that undermines the precedent's foundation [Evidence 12][Evidence 14]. Furthermore, scholarly commentary suggests that administrative deference regimes like *Chevron* have not been treated as binding precedents in the same manner as other substantive decisions, potentially making them more susceptible to overruling [Evidence 13].

**Conclusion**: The Supreme Court in *Loper Bright* overruled the *Chevron* deference doctrine, holding it incompatible with the APA's requirement for independent judicial judgment. Existing case law decided under *Chevron* remains valid, but all future judicial review of agency statutory interpretations must be conducted de novo, with agency views considered only for their persuasive power under *Skidmore*.
