FINAL ANSWER:
================================================================================
**Issue**: What must a claimant prove to acquire title to real property through adverse possession?

**Rule**: To establish title by adverse possession, a claimant must prove their possession of the land was (1) actual and exclusive, (2) open and notorious, (3) adverse (hostile), and (4) continuous for the entire statutory period prescribed by law [Evidence 1][Evidence 2][Evidence 3][Evidence 4][Evidence 5].

**Application**: Each element must be satisfied concurrently for the duration of the statutory period. "Actual and exclusive" possession requires a physical entry and control of the property, meaning the claimant does not share possession with the true owner or the general public [Evidence 1][Evidence 2][Evidence 4]. The exclusivity standard is whether the claimant used the land as the average owner would under the circumstances [Evidence 1][Evidence 2]. "Open and notorious" possession means the use is visible and apparent enough to put a reasonable true owner on notice of the claimant's occupation [Evidence 1][Evidence 2]. "Adverse" or "hostile" possession is established by showing the possession occurred without the true owner's permission or consent [Evidence 1][Evidence 2][Evidence 5]. Under the majority view, the claimant's subjective belief about ownership—whether they knew they were trespassing or mistakenly believed the land was theirs—is irrelevant to this element [Evidence 2][Evidence 3]. Finally, "continuous" possession requires an uninterrupted presence for the full statutory period, with the continuity judged by the standard of use an average owner would make of the property [Evidence 1][Evidence 2].

The research also identifies two ancillary doctrines. First, constructive possession may apply where a claimant actually possesses only part of a tract; possession of the whole tract can be established if there is a reasonable proportion between the part actually possessed and the entire tract [Evidence 3]. Second, regarding the statutory period, an intervening disability of the true owner (e.g., one that arises after adverse possession has begun) does not toll or pause the limitations period. The statute is tolled only if the disability existed *before* the adverse possession commenced [Evidence 2].

**Conclusion**: A claimant acquires title by adverse possession upon proving, for the uninterrupted statutory period, that their possession was actual, exclusive, open and notorious, and hostile to the true owner's title.
