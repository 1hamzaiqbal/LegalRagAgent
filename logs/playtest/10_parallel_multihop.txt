**Issue**: Whether evidence discovered during a warrantless vehicle search must be suppressed when the initial traffic stop was conducted without reasonable suspicion.

**Rule**: The Fourth Amendment requires a traffic stop, a seizure, to be justified by reasonable suspicion—a particularized, objective basis supported by articulable facts that criminal activity may be afoot, assessed under the totality of the circumstances [Evidence 1][Evidence 2][Evidence 5]. A warrantless search of a vehicle is permissible under the automobile exception if police have probable cause to believe it contains contraband or evidence of a crime, as the vehicle's inherent mobility provides the requisite exigency [Evidence 6][Evidence 7][Evidence 9]. The exclusionary rule is a judicial remedy that may require suppression of evidence obtained in violation of the Fourth Amendment, including both primary evidence and its derivative "fruit of the poisonous tree" [Evidence 11][Evidence 13][Evidence 15]. Suppression is not automatic; the rule is triggered only when police conduct is "sufficiently deliberate that exclusion can meaningfully deter it," such as deliberate, reckless, or grossly negligent conduct [Evidence 14][Evidence 15]. Recognized exceptions that may prevent suppression include the good-faith exception, the inevitable discovery doctrine, and the attenuation doctrine [Evidence 11][Evidence 16][Evidence 19].

**Application**: The defendant's strongest arguments are sequential. First, the initial stop was unconstitutional because the officer lacked the requisite reasonable suspicion, rendering the seizure unlawful [Evidence 3][Evidence 4]. Second, any search following this illegal stop is presumptively tainted as "fruit of the poisonous tree" [Evidence 11][Evidence 13]. Even if probable cause for the vehicle search existed independently, the discovery of that probable cause was likely a direct exploitation of the illegal stop, making the drugs subject to suppression.

A court would analyze the exclusionary rule's application in two stages. First, it must determine if a Fourth Amendment violation occurred. Here, a stop without reasonable suspicion is a clear violation [Evidence 2][Evidence 4]. Second, the court would assess whether suppression is the appropriate remedy. The prosecution would likely argue for an exception. The good-faith exception is inapplicable because the officer's conduct—stopping a vehicle without any objective justification—was not based on a reasonable reliance on a warrant, statute, or binding precedent [Evidence 19][Evidence 20]. The attenuation doctrine would also likely fail given the direct temporal and causal link between the illegal stop and the discovery of the drugs [Evidence 19]. The prosecution's most plausible argument is the inevitable discovery doctrine, claiming the drugs would have been found through an independent, lawful investigation. However, they bear the burden of proving this by a preponderance of the evidence [Evidence 17], which would be difficult absent a pre-existing, independent investigation. Finally, the court must consider the nature of the police conduct. A stop without any articulable suspicion suggests at least grossly negligent, if not reckless, conduct, which is precisely the type of behavior the exclusionary rule is designed to deter [Evidence 14][Evidence 15].

**Conclusion**: The defendant's strongest arguments are that the stop violated the Fourth Amendment due to a lack of reasonable suspicion and that the subsequently discovered drugs are the fruit of that unlawful seizure. A court would likely grant the motion to suppress because the constitutional violation is clear and no applicable exception to the exclusionary rule would justify admitting the evidence, given the apparent absence of objectively reasonable good faith or an independent, inevitable line of discovery.
