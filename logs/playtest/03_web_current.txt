**Issue**: What is the current legal status of the Chevron deference doctrine following the Supreme Court's 2024 ruling?

**Rule**: The Chevron deference doctrine, established in *Chevron U.S.A., Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc.*, 467 U.S. 837 (1984), was a foundational principle of administrative law that required courts to defer to a federal agency's reasonable interpretation of an ambiguous statute it administers [Evidence 1][Evidence 3][Evidence 8]. The doctrine employed a two-step framework: first, the court determined if Congress's intent was clear; if not, the court deferred to the agency's permissible construction of the statute [Evidence 4]. This framework was preceded by a "step zero" inquiry to confirm Congress delegated interpretive authority to the agency and the agency acted in exercising that authority [Evidence 5].

**Application**: On June 28, 2024, the Supreme Court overruled the Chevron deference doctrine in its decision in *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo* [Evidence 7][Evidence 9][Evidence 11]. The Court held that the Chevron framework conflicts with the Administrative Procedure Act (APA) [Evidence 11]. Consequently, the rule requiring judicial deference to an agency's reasonable interpretation of statutory ambiguities is no longer good law [Evidence 8][Evidence 10]. The ruling shifts primary interpretive authority from federal agencies to the courts, requiring judges to exercise independent judgment to determine the "best reading" of a statute using traditional tools of statutory construction [Evidence 11][Evidence 12]. The majority rejected arguments based on stare decisis, finding the doctrine "unworkable" and claiming meaningful reliance on it had waned [Evidence 12]. While the overruling does not automatically reopen prior cases decided under Chevron, a party challenging such a ruling would need to establish a "special justification" [Evidence 12]. The dissent strongly criticized the decision for aggrandizing judicial power over expert agencies [Evidence 12].

**Conclusion**: As of June 28, 2024, the Chevron deference doctrine has been overruled and is no longer a valid legal principle. Courts are no longer required to defer to federal agencies' reasonable interpretations of ambiguous statutes they administer and must instead use their independent judgment to interpret the law.
