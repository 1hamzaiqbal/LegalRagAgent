**Issue**: What legal requirements must a California landlord satisfy to lawfully evict a tenant?

**Rule**: California law imposes two primary sets of requirements for a lawful eviction: (1) a substantive "just cause" ground for termination, and (2) strict adherence to procedural steps for notice and court filing.

First, for tenancies where the tenant has continuously and lawfully occupied the property for 12 months (or 24 months under certain conditions), the landlord cannot terminate the tenancy without "just cause," which must be stated in the written termination notice [Evidence 4]. Just cause is divided into two categories. "At-fault just cause" includes tenant actions such as default in rent payment, breach of a material lease term, creating a nuisance, committing waste, criminal activity on the property, unlawful use, unauthorized subletting, or refusal to allow lawful entry [Evidence 4]. For curable at-fault violations, the landlord must first give notice with an opportunity to cure [Evidence 4]. "No-fault just cause" includes the owner's intent to occupy the property (with conditions for leases entered after specific dates), withdrawal of the property from the rental market, compliance with a government order to vacate, or intent to demolish or substantially remodel [Evidence 4]. For no-fault terminations, the landlord must provide relocation assistance or waive the final month's rent [Evidence 4].

Second, these just cause requirements do not apply to several exempt property types, including transient hotels, certain single-family owner-occupied residences, housing built within the last 15 years, and others, provided specific notice requirements are met [Evidence 4]. Furthermore, if a local just cause ordinance was adopted on or before September 1, 2019, or is more protective than state law, the local ordinance applies instead [Evidence 4].

Third, the procedural process for eviction, known as an unlawful detainer action, is mandatory. The landlord must begin by serving a proper written notice on the tenant [Evidence 21][Evidence 22]. For serious, incurable breaches like nuisance, waste, or unlawful use, a "3-Day Notice to Quit" is used, which demands the tenant vacate without an option to cure [Evidence 21]. This notice must include specific details: the full legal names of all tenants, the property address, a precise description of the breach, the termination date, and the landlord's signature [Evidence 21]. The notice must be served using an approved method: personal service (handing it to the tenant), substituted service (leaving it with a suitable person and mailing a copy), or "post and mail" service (posting it on the property and mailing a copy) [Evidence 21]. The three-day period to comply begins the day after personal service; for substituted or post-and-mail service, it begins the day the copy is mailed [Evidence 21].

**Application**: To evict a tenant, a landlord must first determine if the tenancy is subject to just cause requirements based on the tenant's length of occupancy and the property's exempt status [Evidence 4]. If just cause applies, the landlord must identify a permissible at-fault or no-fault ground, provide any required opportunity to cure or relocation assistance, and state the cause in the termination notice [Evidence 4]. Regardless of just cause applicability, the landlord must then serve a legally sufficient written notice (such as a 3-Day Notice to Quit for incurable violations) following strict service rules [Evidence 21]. If the tenant does not comply by the notice deadline, the landlord may commence a court action by filing an Unlawful Detainer Complaint in Superior Court, attaching the notice and a Proof of Service [Evidence 21][Evidence 22]. The tenant must then be served with a summons and the complaint [Evidence 21]. A landlord cannot use "self-help" measures like changing locks or removing belongings; they must obtain a court judgment and a Writ of Possession, which instructs the sheriff to carry out the eviction [Evidence 22].

**Conclusion**: A California landlord must (1) have a permissible "just cause" ground for termination if the tenancy is covered by state or local just cause laws, (2) serve a technically correct written notice using an approved service method, and (3) file and win an unlawful detainer lawsuit in court to obtain a writ for the sheriff to execute the eviction.
