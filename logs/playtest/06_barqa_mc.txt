**Issue**: Whether the defendant's use of a toy gun to intimidate a cashier into handing over money provides a viable defense to a charge of common law robbery.

**Rule**: Common law robbery is defined as the felonious taking and carrying away of personal property from another's person or presence by violence or putting in fear [Evidence 1][Evidence 2][Evidence 3]. The essential "force or threat of force" element distinguishes robbery from theft and can be satisfied by either actual violence or constructive force, also termed intimidation [Evidence 2]. Intimidation occurs when the defendant's conduct places the victim in fear, compelling them to part with their property [Evidence 2][Evidence 3]. Critically, to secure a robbery conviction, the State is **not** required to prove: (1) the victim reasonably feared *immediate* physical harm, (2) the defendant intended to create such fear, or (3) the defendant had the apparent ability to inflict physical harm [Evidence 7][Evidence 8]. A mere oral threat of force that puts another in fear can be sufficient [Evidence 8]. The research did not address the specific intent to permanently deprive.

**Application**: The defendant's strongest defense must attack an element the prosecution is required to prove. Here, the prosecution must prove a taking by "force or threat of force." The evidence establishes that intimidation—putting the victim in fear—is sufficient to meet this element [Evidence 2][Evidence 3]. The cashier, believing the toy gun was real, handed over the cash, demonstrating the defendant's actions successfully placed the victim in fear. Under the governing law, the prosecution does **not** need to prove the object used for intimidation was actually dangerous, that the defendant intended to create fear of immediate harm, or that the defendant had the apparent ability to cause such harm [Evidence 7][Evidence 8]. Therefore, arguments based on the toy gun's nature (A), the absence of physical harm (B), or the lack of actual force (C) are not defenses, as they target elements not required for common law robbery. The evidence does not specify whether the prosecution must prove a specific intent to permanently deprive, so the strength of argument (D) cannot be assessed based on the provided research.

**Conclusion**: Based solely on the provided evidence, none of the arguments (A), (B), or (C) constitute a valid defense to common law robbery because they challenge elements not required by law. The research did not address the specific intent element, so the viability of argument (D) is unknown. Among the arguments the evidence does address, (A), (B), and (C) are all legally insufficient.

**Answer: (D)**
