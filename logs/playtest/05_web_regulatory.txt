FINAL ANSWER:
================================================================================
**Issue**: What are the current federal regulations requiring publicly traded companies to disclose Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) information?

**Rule**: The current federal regulatory framework for ESG disclosures is not a standalone, prescriptive rule but is grounded in the existing securities laws and the foundational concept of financial materiality [Evidence 22][Evidence 24]. Disclosure obligations arise primarily from specific items in Regulation S-K, which governs the non-financial statement content of SEC filings [Evidence 11][Evidence 22]. First, Item 303 of Regulation S-K requires a company to disclose in its Management’s Discussion and Analysis (MD&A) any known trends or uncertainties that management reasonably expects will have a material effect on financial condition or results of operations [Evidence 8][Evidence 13][Evidence 14]. Second, Item 105 requires the disclosure of material risks to the company's operations in a "risk factors" section, written in plain English [Evidence 9]. Third, Item 101 requires a description of human capital resources to the extent material to understanding the business [Evidence 24]. For climate-related risks specifically, the SEC's 2010 interpretive guidance remains applicable, identifying where such material issues must be disclosed under Regulation S-K [Evidence 18][Evidence 23]. All such disclosures are subject to the general anti-fraud provisions of the securities laws, and misleading statements can trigger liability [Evidence 20][Evidence 24].

**Application**: Applying this framework, a publicly traded company must assess whether any specific ESG issue is financially material. An environmental trend like climate change, a social issue like workforce management, or a governance matter like board oversight must be disclosed if it meets the materiality thresholds under Items 303 or 105 [Evidence 8][Evidence 9]. For example, a known climate-related risk that is reasonably likely to affect future revenues must be discussed in the MD&A [Evidence 8][Evidence 13]. Material human capital issues, such as labor shortages critical to operations, must be described under Item 101, though no specific metrics are mandated [Evidence 24]. The research did not address a federal rule mandating specific board diversity disclosures. While the SEC adopted a dedicated climate disclosure rule in March 2024 requiring specific disclosures like material Scope 1 and Scope 2 greenhouse gas emissions [Evidence 1][Evidence 3][Evidence 7], that rule is not in effect. Its implementation is stayed due to litigation, and the SEC has withdrawn its defense, making its future under the current Commission unlikely [Evidence 2][Evidence 4][Evidence 6]. Therefore, companies currently rely on the principles-based, materiality-driven existing regulations.

**Conclusion**: Currently, there is no prescriptive federal ESG disclosure rule in effect for publicly traded companies. Instead, ESG disclosures are required under existing SEC regulations—specifically Items 303, 105, and 101 of Regulation S-K—when the information is determined to be financially material to a reasonable investor. The SEC's 2024 climate-specific disclosure rule has been stayed and is not being defended, leaving the materiality-based framework as the governing standard.
