**Issue**: What must a plaintiff prove to establish a prima facie claim for negligence?

**Rule**: A plaintiff must prove four essential elements: (1) a duty of care owed by the defendant to the plaintiff, (2) a breach of that duty, (3) causation (both actual and proximate) of injury, and (4) damages [Evidence 2][Evidence 4][Evidence 5]. The duty is a legal obligation to conform to a specific standard of conduct for the protection of others against unreasonable risks [Evidence 1][Evidence 2]. Under established doctrine, this standard is generally that of a reasonably prudent person under similar circumstances [Evidence 2][Evidence 3]. Breach is the failure to conform to this required standard [Evidence 1][Evidence 3]. Causation requires the breach to be both the actual cause (cause in fact) and the proximate (legal) cause of the plaintiff's injury [Evidence 2][Evidence 5]. Finally, the plaintiff must have suffered actual damage to person or property [Evidence 2][Evidence 4].

**Application**: These elements form a sequential framework for analyzing any negligence claim. The existence of a duty is the threshold question [Evidence 5]. Once duty is established, the plaintiff must demonstrate how the defendant's specific conduct fell below the standard of care, constituting a breach [Evidence 1][Evidence 3]. The plaintiff must then connect this breach to their harm through causation, proving both that the injury would not have occurred "but for" the breach (actual cause) and that the injury was a foreseeable consequence of the breach (proximate cause) [Evidence 2][Evidence 5]. The final element, damages, requires proof of a legally recognized injury; nominal damages are insufficient [Evidence 4]. While the core framework is consistent, its application can vary by context. For example, in a medical malpractice suit, expert testimony is typically required to articulate the standard of care and establish causation [Evidence 1].

**Conclusion**: To succeed on a negligence claim, a plaintiff must prove, by a preponderance of the evidence, all four elements: duty, breach, causation, and damages.
