**Issue**: What legal theories are available to a homeowner who hired a contractor for a defective kitchen remodel, and what must be proven for each?

**Rule**: The homeowner may pursue claims for breach of contract, negligence, and breach of an implied warranty of workmanlike quality [Evidence 8][Evidence 11][Evidence 22][Evidence 26].

For a **breach of contract** claim, the homeowner must prove: (1) the existence of a valid contract, which itself requires offer and acceptance, consideration, and definite and certain terms [Evidence 1][Evidence 2][Evidence 3]; (2) performance by the plaintiff of all required conditions [Evidence 1]; (3) a breach by the defendant, defined as any nonperformance when performance is due [Evidence 4]; and (4) damages resulting from the breach [Evidence 1]. A breach is material if it substantially deprives the homeowner of the contract's expected benefit, considering factors like the extent of deprivation and the adequacy of compensation [Evidence 4]. An immaterial breach, where the contractor has substantially performed, does not discharge the homeowner's obligations [Evidence 4][Evidence 5].

For a **negligence** claim, the homeowner must prove: (1) the contractor owed a duty of reasonable care to protect against unreasonable risks [Evidence 8][Evidence 10]; (2) the contractor breached that duty by failing to conform to the required standard of care [Evidence 6][Evidence 8]; (3) the breach was both the actual and proximate cause of the injury [Evidence 7][Evidence 8]; and (4) the homeowner suffered damage to property [Evidence 7][Evidence 8].

For a **breach of the implied warranty of workmanlike quality**, the homeowner must establish that the contractor failed to perform the remodel services in a "good and workmanlike manner" [Evidence 22]. This warranty is implied by law in contracts for services to repair or modify existing property, arising as a matter of public policy to protect consumer expectations, and applies even in the absence of a written warranty [Evidence 18][Evidence 22][Evidence 24][Evidence 26]. The research did not specify a precise list of elements for proving breach, but the warranty is actionable under common law [Evidence 16][Evidence 18]. This warranty for work on existing property is distinct from the implied warranty of habitability, which applies to new home construction [Evidence 15][Evidence 19].

**Application**: The homeowner alleging a defective remodel using substandard materials has facts supporting each theory. For breach of contract, the homeowner must show the remodeling agreement was a valid contract and that using substandard materials constituted a breach, likely a material one given the defective work [Evidence 4]. For negligence, the homeowner must demonstrate the contractor's duty of reasonable care in performing construction services and that using substandard materials breached that duty, causing the defective kitchen and resulting damages [Evidence 6][Evidence 8]. For breach of the implied warranty, the homeowner must prove the work was not done in a good and workmanlike manner, which the use of substandard materials and defective workmanship would likely demonstrate [Evidence 22]. The homeowner can assert this warranty claim even without a written contract term guaranteeing quality [Evidence 24].

**Conclusion**: The homeowner may sue for breach of contract, negligence, and breach of the implied warranty of workmanlike quality. Each theory requires proof of distinct elements: contract formation and breach; duty, breach, causation, and damages; and failure to perform in a good and workmanlike manner, respectively.
